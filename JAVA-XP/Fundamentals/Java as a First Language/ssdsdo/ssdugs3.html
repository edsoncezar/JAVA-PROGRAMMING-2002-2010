Short introduction to the unit

Return to top

Software Development is a difficult and complex process and learning how to develop software is equally difficult and complex. Most students report that software development is the most difficult of their first year classes. Software Development is both an academic body of knowledge and a practical skill. Just like other practical skills, such as riding a bicycle or driving a car, it is not possible to learn the skill by reading about how to do it.

The only way to learn
Software Development
is to
Develop Software!!

The skills and knowledge involved in software development are fundamental to your studies in the second and final years; and in particular is vital for your final year project. When you graduate in computing you may never be required ever to actually develop a software artifact, but every employer will expect that you have a practical understanding of the process and minimal skills in the process.

Even when you complete a computing degree you will only be qualified to work as a junior developer and will require several years experience and practice to progress. If you look at the pages of job adverts that appear in national papers and in the weekly Computing newspaper you will discover that the vast majority of the jobs on offer require both software development skills and practical experience.

Aims of the unit

Return to top

The principal aim of this course is to provide a practical introduction to modern software development theory, practice and techniques. Software is the product that defines the computer industry and an understanding of the processes by which software is developed is an essential requirement for anyone who hopes to work in the computer industry.

This is recognized in the course regulations which place a particular emphasis upon students successfully completing both first year software development units (this unit and the semester 2 unit Software Development with Objects CSD-1-SDO) before proceeding to year 2. Software development units will continue to be a core theme throughout the remainder of the computing courses in the school.

The learning outcomes for the unit

  1. Use a simple integrated software development environment.
  2. Use appropriate graphical notations to describe and design software.
  3. Demonstrate that artifacts appear to be implemented correctly.
  4. Practice development by extension.
  5. Employ appropriate features of an OO language to implement requirements.
  6. Produce documentation describing both the product and its production process.
  7. Make suitable use of existing resources.
  8. Log and reflect upon individual learning progress.

Key and cognitive skills

Return to top
Specifically by the end of this course you should be able to:
  1. operate an NT workstation and manage your NT environment.
  2. produce a program design from a program specification.
  3. implement the program design making use of pre-supplied components.
  4. demonstrate that the implementation conforms with the specification.
  5. produce documentation to support and describe the software produced.
  6. extend the facilities of a pre-supplied software component.

Introduction to studying this unit

Software Development is a complex skill that can only be perfected by practical activity. To this end you will be expected to spend much of your time designing and implementing software. Only one hour a week will be used for lectures, the remaining three hours contact time will be devoted to lab-based workshops. You will be also expected to spend at least another four hours a week practicing these skills.

Syllabus

This unit will run for 15 weeks starting in February 2002 and ending in June 2002. The first 13 weeks will be contact weeks the remaining 2 will be used for assessment.

The majority of the time allocation for the unit will be spent in workshops, where you are expected to progress through your learning plan at your own pace. It is your responsibility to keep your tutor informed of your progress and in particular to ensure that you are formally assessed at the waypoints.

Communication

At the start of the unit you will be assigned to a software development tutor who will staff the workshop and who will assess your progress. The tutor's availability will be made clear during the first meeting.

The team as opposed to your tutor will communicate with you via messages on course notice boards, e- mail into your account and/ or messages on the Web and discussion group. We will assume that a message sent by any of these methods has been received by the entire group.

Exam format and instructions

Return to top

The examination will be administered on-line and will consist of 50 multiple choice questions.

The waypoint 1 & 2 material has a number of on-line self-test multiple choice quizzes. The questions in the examination will be similar to those provided. The best way to prepare for the examination is to practice on the self-test quizzes.

Waypoints

There are three defined waypoints in the SSD unit. We expect you to reach these waypoints at approximately weeks 5, 10 and 12. It is more important that you have thoroughly understood all the material and completed all the practical exercises, before the waypoint assessment; than to attempt to keep to a schedule which is too fast for you. However it is also necessary for you to complete all three waypoints by the end of the semester!

To pass waypoints 1 and 2 you have to present your logbook which provides evidence that you have completed all activities and will contain a summary sheet which you fill out to show this. You will also have to take a short test and to complete a practical development exercise, during a lab session.

Waypoint 1 and waypoint 2 have 10 marks for the logbook, 5 marks for the test and 15 marks for the practical exercise. You should aim to get at least 20 marks in total. The table below gives the dates when the waypoint 1 & 2 assessments will be conducted.

Waypoint 3 consists of a complete design, build and document exercise and is worth 40 marks.

Early Referral

In recognition of the fundamental nature of the skills taught by this unit the B.Sc. Computing course regulations allow for an early referral in software development. Students who do not pass this unit in semester 2 will not be allowed to take the second semester Software Development with Objects (CSD-1-SDO) in semester 3, but will be required to re-take this unit in semester 3.

Students who fail either the coursework or examination part of the assessment will be offered an opportunity to redeem the failure in the last week of semester 2 or the first week of semester 3. This will involve retaking the examination and completing a lab-based practical assessment.

Students who fail this unit in semester 2 will be expected to redeem the failure by re-attempting the waypoint and examination assessments in semester 3.

Logbooks

Return to top

Your are expected to maintain a logbook. In it you should make an entry every time you do some work on a terminal which contributes towards a waypoint. The entry should:

  1. Indicate what you intend to achieve.
  2. Indicate what you did to achieve it.
  3. Indicate what problems you had achieving it.
  4. Indicate what questions you have on what you have done.
  5. Indicate what you intend to do next.

Your logbook should not be a notebook. That is it should not contain summaries from the text book or from the web material. It should not contain program designs or listings or scripts of program runs. It is an executive summary of your learning activities.

The logbook must be a live document which records your own work!

You should make an entry in the logbook every time you do a significant activity connected with the unit, not just during the weekly practical classes. You should bring your logbook to every practical class so that you tutor can assess and sign it. Logbooks will no be assessed or signed at any other time. An example logbook entry might be as follows. (This example is illustrative not perscriptive!)
