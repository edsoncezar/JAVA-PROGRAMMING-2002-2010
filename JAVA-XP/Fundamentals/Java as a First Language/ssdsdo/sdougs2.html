Introduction : Learning by doing!

This unit will continue the theme, organisation and structure
of the preceding SSD unit. Insofar as it is possible you will have
the same tutor as in the first semester.

You are reminded that Software Development is both an academic body of knowledge and a practical skill. Just like other practical skills, such as riding a bicycle or driving a car, it is not possible to learn the skill by reading about how to do it.

The only way to learn Software Development
is to Develop Software!!

The truth of this statement should be clear from your previous Software Development unit(s) and the necessity of scheduling your time so as to be able to make the most effective use of the learning resources should also be apparent.

Aims and Objectives

The initial level of knowledge and skill provided by the preceding SSD unit will be further developed in this unit. Software development is seen as an intellectual skill and the development of skills requires continual practise and extension. Several additional aspects will be introduced in this unit allowing artefacts to communicate more effectively with their environment and to operate concurrently.

Learning Outcomes

On completion of this unit the student should be able to:

  • Appreciate the needs of a human user and design appropriately.
  • Use and implement objects which contain a number of other objects.
  • Output the state of an object, in a number of different ways.
  • Input the state of an object which has previously been output.
  • Implement a simple object which can safely operate concurrently.
  • Implement an object which can respond to the receipt of events.
  • Describe an artefact which has a GUI in terms of a three-layer model.
  • Implement and demonstrate a simple GUI driven artifact.

Studying this unit

Software Development is a complex skill that can only be perfected by practical activity. To this end you will be expected to spend much of your time designing and implementing software. Only one hour a week will be used for lectures, the remaining three hours contact time will be devoted to lab-based workshops. You will be also expected to spend at least another four hours a week practising these skills.

Organisation and structure

This unit will run for 15 weeks starting in February 2000 and ending in May 2000. The first 12 weeks will be contact weeks the remaining 3 will be used for assessment.

The majority of the time allocation for the unit will be spent in workshops, where you are expected to progress through your learning plan at your own pace. It is your responsibility to keep your tutor informed of your progress and in particular to ensure that you are formally assessed at the waypoints.

Communications

At the start of the unit you will be assigned to a software development tutor who will staff the workshop and who will assess your progress. The tutor's availability will be made clear during the first meeting.

The team as opposed to your tutor will communicate with you via messages on course notice boards, e- mail into your account and/ or messages on the Web and discussion group. We will assume that a message sent by any of these methods has been received by the entire group.

Resources

The set book for this, and its follow-on unit SDO, is 'Java: An Object First Approach', Fintan Culwin, Prentice Hall 1998, ISBN 0-13-858457-5.

The majority of the material which you will need is available on the SBU Web server. During your first sessions your tutor will help you to get started with accessing and using this material.

There is an account on the CSD Unix machine called jfl, which contains example programs and other documents which are available for student use. You will be directed by the lecturers and tutors to look for certain files in the team area when they are required.

A free Java development environment (Sun JDK) and program editor (PFE), is available. To run it you will need a pentium pc with at least 8 Megs of RAM and about 30 Meg spare disc space running Windows 95 or NT. The easiest way to obtain it is to buy a copy of the BURKS 4 CD (£5.00), available from Blackwell’s bookshop. (This CD also contains a large number of other resources that you might find useful throughout your degree.)

Waypoints

There are three defined waypoints in the SSD unit. We expect you to reach these waypoints at approximately weeks 4, 9 and 12. It is more important that you have thoroughly understood all the material and completed all the practical exercises, before you ask for a waypoint assessment; than to attempt to keep to a schedule which is too fast for you. However it is also necessary for you to complete all three waypoints by the end of the semester!

To pass waypoints 4 and 6 you have to present your workbook which provides evidence that you have completed all activities and will contain a summary sheet which you fill out to show this. You will also have to take a short test and to complete a practical development exercise, during a lab session.

Waypoint 4 and waypoint 6 have 10 marks for the workbook, 10 marks for the test and 10 marks for the practical exercise. To pass a waypoint you have to get at least 20 marks in total. The table below gives the last date by which you must submit for your waypoint 4 & 6 assessments. If you obtain less than 20 marks on your first attempt at either of these assessments you can have a second attempt in the following week, so long as this is not later than the last allowed date, to obtain a maximum of 20 marks.

Waypoint 5 consists of a complete design, build and document exercise and is worth 40 marks.

Workbooks

Your are expected to maintain a workbook. In it you should make an entry every time you do some work on a terminal which contributes towards a waypoint. The entry should:

  • Indicate what you intend to achieve.
  • Indicate what you did to achieve it.
  • Indicate what problems you had achieving it.
  • Indicate what questions you have on what you have done.
  • Indicate what you intend to do next.

    • Your workbook should not be a notebook. That is it should not contain summaries from the text book or from the web material. It should not contain program designs or listings or scripts of program runs. It is an executive summary of your learning activities.
